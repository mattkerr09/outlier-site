And one more thing

Local AI is better for the planet.

This isn't a marketing line. The numbers are real, and they're getting worse fast. Every query that runs on your Mac instead of someone's H100 rack is a query that didn't need a gigawatt datacenter.

1,050 TWh

Projected global data center electricity use by end of 2026 — roughly Japan's entire annual consumption.

80–90%

Share of AI energy consumption driven by inference, not training. Every query counts.

~10×

Energy used by a single ChatGPT-style query compared to a standard Google search.

The inference layer is where AI energy demand is exploding. A single question to a cloud model means booting up a GPU rack, streaming the prompt through a network fabric, running it, streaming the answer back. The electricity comes from a grid that's increasingly struggling to keep up. Virginia's data centers already consume more than a quarter of the state's electricity. Ireland's are projected to hit 32% of national consumption by end of 2026.

Apple Silicon does the same work on a fraction of the power. Unified memory means no PCIe transfers, no NVLink fabric, no cooling towers. An M-series Mac running a 7B model draws about as much electricity as a laptop charger. And it comes from your house, not a datacenter displacing a town's grid capacity.

Is Outlier going to single-handedly fix the climate impact of AI? No. But if most inference shifts to the edge — where the chips are already efficient and already running — the trend line bends. That's the argument. It's real, and it's the one part of this whole thing that isn't about software.

Sources: IEA Global Energy Review 2025 · MIT News energy analysis · Apple 2025 environmental report

Questions

Before you download.

Is it really free? What's the catch?
No catch. The app is free forever for personal and commercial use. Base model weights are Apache 2.0 (licensed by the Qwen team at Alibaba). Pro tier will ship later with power-user features like agentic coding mode, long-context mode, and voice input; when it does, the free tier you downloaded today keeps working unchanged. If you want to help fund the roadmap sooner, the Support section has one-time and monthly options.
What is the actual goal? Where is this going?
The goal is an offline model that codes as well as the cloud flagships — running entirely on a consumer Mac. We aren't there yet. Nobody is. But the gap is closing fast, and Outlier exists to close it. Four phases, no timelines. See the Mission section for the plan.
Why $200 for Founders? What happens after 500 seats?
Founders is a single one-time $200 payment, lifetime-locked. The 500-seat cap is hard — after it fills, Founders closes and support becomes Pro at $20/month (unlimited use, all Pro features). The lifetime benefit for Founders stays with you forever. Pricing this way rewards early supporters and gives the rest of the roadmap a funded runway. We're transparent about every piece of this.
What Mac do I need?
Any Apple Silicon Mac (M1 / M2 / M3 / M4) running macOS 13 or later. Nano tier runs on 8 GB RAM. Lite and Compact work on 16 GB+. Max and Code need 32 GB+. Intel Macs are not supported — Apple Silicon only.
How does it compare to Ollama or LM Studio?
Ollama is a CLI for developers and recently added a paid cloud tier. LM Studio is a generalist GUI built on Electron. Outlier is Mac-native Tauri (8.8 MB vs ~200 MB), curated tiers instead of a model zoo, strictly local, and staying free at the base tier. All three use similar backends — the difference is polish, curation, pricing philosophy, and the direction of travel. Outlier is pointed at Phase 4.
Does my data really stay on my Mac?
Yes. All inference runs on your Mac's GPU via MLX. Prompts, context, and outputs never leave the machine. Web search is opt-in and labeled when in use. Unplug the ethernet and airplane-mode the Wi-Fi — Outlier keeps working.
Is local AI actually better for the environment?
Yes, and the numbers back it up. Inference drives 80–90% of AI energy consumption. Cloud inference means running GPU racks, network fabric, and cooling; local inference on Apple Silicon uses a fraction of the power because unified memory eliminates most of that overhead. Won't fix climate single-handedly, but every query that runs on your Mac is one that didn't need a datacenter. See the Energy section for sources.
What is Outlier-Coder 30B, exactly?
It's our Mac-optimized build of Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B-Instruct — a strong open-weights coding model from the Alibaba Qwen team, released under Apache 2.0. We quantize it to MLX 4-bit (16 GB on disk instead of 61 GB), tune the sampling defaults for Apple Silicon, and bundle it into the Outlier installer. The base model is the work of the Qwen team; we credit them prominently. Our own coder (Phase 3) will eventually replace this tier.
What's the "research track" about?
Our own ternary Mixture-of-Experts family (10B–150B scales) trained as overlay deltas on a frozen Qwen base. Three U.S. provisional patents filed. When a research scale validates at a real Mac RAM tier, it graduates into the shipping stack. The research track is the technical on-ramp to Phase 4.
Who built this?
Matt Kerr, solo. Kerr & Company LLC (DBA Outlier), Grand Rapids, MI. Built in 19 days on a Mac Studio with under $1,200 of total compute spend. Three U.S. provisional patents on the architecture. Direct line: matt@outlier.host.
What happens when a better open model drops?
We rebase. Our pipeline absorbs the next Qwen, Llama, or DeepSeek release in weeks. The free tier you have today keeps getting updated open-weights models as they ship — continuously. No retraining required on your end.
